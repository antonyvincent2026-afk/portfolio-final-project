Source Credibility Crisis in Journalism: The Role of AI

Illustration of Artificial Intelligence integrated in modern newsrooms, Credit: Image via Easy-Peasy.AI,Licensed under CC BY-ND 4.0 .

Sources are a crucial aspect of journalism as they drive the narrative of any story. However, they present a double-edged sword. If the sources are not properly vetted, one bad source could damage newsroom reputation that took years to build. It could also potentially cause legal, institutional, and financial risk.

During my primary research, I found out that journalists don't fail because they can't find any sources. Rather, their fail is driven by a lack of a structured system to vet sources and evaluate the risks associated with them before quoting them in a story. The harsh reality about newsrooms is that strict deadlines force fast decisions. However, source vetting is still a manual process largely reliant on manual checks and personal intutions. This presents an inconsistent, informal and time-consuming process.

Professor Tim Franklin at Northwestern University highlighted how the issues associated with source vetting has aggrevated in recent years and is at an all-time high in this era where artificial intelligence is transforming the media landscape.

A major risk of AI in journalism is not just potential misinformation, but authority that can be performed. Professor Jeremy Gilbert, Knight professor in digital media strategy at Northwestern University, highlights the risk of reliance on generative AI as a research source. He wxplains in the attached soundcite clip how AI uses a language of authority and expertise to masquerade as an expert. Click play to listen to the audio

This observation holds signficant relevance today as journalists increasingly use AI tools for support in preliminary research. Although the output sounds polished, it creates a false sense of safety as they hold the risk of AI hallucinations.

However, despite these risks,both faculty members emphasized that AI still has a place in journalism as long as it does not replace human judgement.

Artificial Intelligence and Human Brain, Credit: Wikimedia Commons

Professor Glibert highlighted two unique ways AI could responsibly support journalists without replacing editorial judgement, Firstly, AI could be used as a tool to help journalists identiy new potential sources. He emphasized that instead of replicating google searches, AI could be effectively leveraged to analyze the sourcers other journalists have cited on similar topics. Furthermore, he believes AI could be used to recommend additional experts that are potentially overlooked in that specific field. Hence, AI could be an effective tool to expand the pool of sources to help journalists oncover new voices and perspectives about various subjects.

Secondly, Professor Gilbert also suggested that AI can be used to conduct internal checks on sourcing. Therefore, after a journalist drafts a story, AI can be used to run background checks on the sources used and prompt to the reporter to check whether there is a missing pespective. Professor Gilbert believes that this AI tool could effectively safeguard and strengthen reporting without replacing human judgment.

Professor Franklin further highlighted areas of journalism where AI should not be used.

Professor Gilbert echoed a similar sentiment as he highlighted the importance of intellectual ownership of output.

In conclusion, the source credibility crisis in journalism is not due to lack of values, but an absence of a system that matches today's dynamic and complex world of information overload. AI can effectively be used to combat the crisis if used with accountability and transparency. However, in an era where anyone and anything can sound like an expert, misuse of AI could detrimentally impact the future of journalism. Therefore, the future of journalism depends on embracing technology and using it responsibly.
